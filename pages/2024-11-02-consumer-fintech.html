Business models in consumer fintech

There are only 5 common business models in consumer fintech. They are:

  • Net interest income
  • Credit/debit card interchange
  • Loans
  • Fees
  • Subscriptions

Despite the jargon, these are simple to understand.

Net Interest Income

You keep money in an account and the company takes your money, invests it in money market funds, and earns interest (yield). Sometimes the company shares some of this yield with you, sometimes they don't. This revenue tends to be pure profit for them - there are approximately zero costs to moving customer deposits into short-term, risk-free monetary instruments.

Credit/debit card interchange

Every time you use your credit or debit card, the issuer gets between ~1-2% of the amount in revenue [2]. Some issuers will rebate a portion of that as cash back, points, miles etc and keep the rest. The exact take is hard to know and depends on the type of card used, purchase category, whether the issuer is "Durbin-exempt", and the negotiated rate the fintech has with their card issuer. The last point is worth elaborating on - most fintechs with debit/credit cards are not technically banks and therefore rely on partner banks to issue the card.

Loans

I'm grouping several types of loans under this umbrella. The premise is simple - you're borrowing money and paying interest on the amount owed. The mechanism are varied: margin loan against your stock portfolio, credit card balance, personal/student/car/mortage loans etc. etc. Interest rates can be fixed or variable, the loans may be amortized (you start paying back the principal right away) or not (you only pay interest for the duration of the loan and then pay back the principal as a lump sum at the end). A interest-only period, followed by amortization is also possible. The institution will earn an origination fee and a spread on the interest paid by you and the interest paid by them (they're not using their own capital, they're borrowing from someone else and lending to you at a markup).

Fees

Another broad category. Depending on company's specialization they may charge different fees. These can be: trading fees (Coinbase), foreign transaction fees, transfer fees (same-day withdrawals, wires etc), miscellaneous fees (overdraft, account maintenance fees), cash withdrawal fees, assets-under-management (AUM) fee etc. The industry has been shifting away from fees due to a mix of competitive pressures (eg Robinhood's fee-free trading, Chime removing all fees) and regulation (CFPB Junk Fees Proposal). But despite that, this category remains substantial - overdraft fees alone are estimated to be ~$9 billion per year.

Subscriptions

This is becoming more common today as fintechs choose to forego more revenue from the previous categories for customers who pay a membership fee. Examples include Robinhood Gold, Coinbase One, Mercury Personal.

As an exercise for the reader, I recommend opening your favorite banking/brokerage/payments app and bucketing where you think the revenue falls into. It's surprisingly easy and given the fintech convergence (a topic for a future article) in the last 10 years, each company has a mix of income from several of these categories. This exercise isn't just fun, it also helps one understand their relationship with the firm, and the balance of that relationship. It's also a way to identify deals that are "too good to be true" and are likely to be finite in length or even may be short term.
